## 1. Introduction & Scope

This document extends the baseline 2D composite-beam analysis (see `numerical_results.md`) with five additional analyses to provide a more complete evaluation of straight vs. curved (bow-matched) intramedullary nails for atypical femoral fractures (AFF):

1. **Frontal-plane (coronal) bending** — superposition of the dominant medial-lateral hip bending moment
2. **Neutral-axis compatibility index** — a dimensionless metric for nail-bone curvature mismatch
3. **Two-segment EI model** — stiffness discontinuity at the nail tip and its effect on local curvature
4. **Strain and fatigue interpretation** — relating stresses to yield strain and fatigue endurance limit
5. **Multi-objective comparison** — synthesizing all metrics to compare straight vs. curved nail designs

**Key limitations:** All analyses remain within Euler-Bernoulli beam theory and simple superposition. No explicit contact mechanics, muscle forces, or 3D FEA are used. The model captures load-sharing and section-level stress/strain but not localized contact stresses, stress concentrations at screw holes, or anterior cortex impingement.

## 3. Frontal-Plane (Coronal) Bending and Superposition

### 3.1 Rationale

The hip joint contact force acts through the femoral head, which is medially offset from the femoral shaft axis by the **femoral offset** d_ML. This creates a frontal-plane bending moment at the subtrochanteric / mid-diaphyseal level that is typically *larger* than the sagittal bow-induced moment [8], [9].

**Femoral offset (d_ML):**

| Parameter | Nominal | Range | Units | Sources |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| d_ML (hip center to shaft axis) | 48 | 35-65 | mm | Tatka et al. 2022 (mean 38 mm, cadaveric); Lechler et al. 2014 (rotation-corrected mean 57 mm); Biggi et al. 2020 |

### 3.2 Frontal-Plane Bending Moment

$$M_{\text{front}} = N \cdot d_{ML}$$

M_front = 1680 x 48 = **80640 N*mm**

Compare to sagittal moment: M_sag = 30545 N*mm

**Ratio M_front / M_sag = 2.64** — the frontal-plane moment is 2.6x larger than the sagittal bow-induced moment.

### 3.3 Frontal-Plane Section Properties

For a circular cross-section, I is identical regardless of bending axis. In the frontal plane, the nail sits centrally within the medullary canal (y_n = 0 in the M-L direction) for **both** straight and curved nails. Therefore:

- I_comp,front (both nailed cases) = I_b + n * I_n = 24597 mm^4
- c_lat,front = r_o = 13.0 mm (lateral cortex is outermost in M-L)

### 3.4 Frontal-Plane Stresses

| Case | sigma_front (MPa) | I used (mm^4) |
|---|---|---|
| Bone only | 52.56 | 19947 (I_b) |
| Straight nail | 42.62 | 24597 (I_comp,front) |
| Curved nail | 42.62 | 24597 (I_comp,front) |

**Key insight:** In the frontal plane, both nail types produce identical bending stress because the nail is centered (no mismatch). The straight nail's sagittal advantage (lower sigma_sag due to NA shift) does *not* carry over to the frontal plane.

### 3.5 Combined Stress (Sagittal + Frontal)

Both sagittal and frontal bending produce tension at the lateral cortex. Using scalar superposition (conservative upper bound):

$$\sigma_{\text{lat,total}} = \sigma_{\text{lat,sag}} + \sigma_{\text{front}}$$

**Observations:**
- Frontal-plane bending dominates: it contributes 52.6 MPa (bone) vs 15.2 MPa (sagittal) — **78%** of total stress for bone only.
- The straight and curved nails now show **nearly identical** total stress (55.07 vs 57.03 MPa), because the frontal component dominates and is the same for both.
- The straight nail's sagittal advantage is diluted: it was 17.9% lower in sagittal-only, but only 18.7% lower in the combined stress.
- **Bone only** (FOS = 1.48) now falls **below** FOS = 2.0, highlighting why a nail is biomechanically necessary. Both nailed cases maintain FOS > 1.5.

## 4. Neutral-Axis Compatibility Index

### 4.1 Definition

The **compatibility index** C quantifies how eccentric the nail centroid is within the medullary canal, as a fraction of the bone radius:

$$C = \frac{|y_n|}{r_o}$$

where y_n is the nail centroid offset from the bone centroid (in the sagittal plane) due to curvature mismatch.

- C = 0: nail perfectly follows the bone curvature (ideal)
- C > 0: nail deviates from the canal centerline, increasing anterior cortex contact risk

### 4.2 Results

| Scenario | Nail Type | y_n (mm) | C = |y_n|/r_o | sigma_total (MPa) | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nominal (R_f=1100) | Straight | 1.89 | 0.146 | 55.07 | Moderate mismatch |
| Nominal (R_f=1100) | Curved | 0.00 | 0.000 | 57.03 | Perfect match |
| Asian female (R_f=880) | Straight | 3.67 | 0.282 | 42.04 | Larger mismatch |
| Asian female (R_f=880) | Curved | 0.00 | 0.000 | 45.69 | Perfect match |

### 4.3 Interpretation

- For the nominal population, C_straight = 0.146 — the nail centroid sits **1.9 mm** anterior to the bone centroid.
- For Asian females with R_f = 880 mm, C_straight = 0.282 — the mismatch grows to **3.7 mm**, nearly 1.9x worse.
- A non-zero C means the straight nail presses against the anterior cortex, creating **localized contact stresses** not captured by beam theory.
- The curved nail has C = 0 by design, eliminating this mismatch-driven cortical contact.
- Even though the straight nail has marginally lower sigma_total in the beam model, **the compatibility index reveals the hidden risk**: anterior cortex impingement, which is the primary clinical mechanism of nail-related AFF [12], [15], [19], [20].

## 5. Tip Stiffness Discontinuity (Two-Segment EI Model)

### 5.1 Concept

An intramedullary nail creates two distinct flexural regions along the femoral shaft:

- **Region 1 (nailed):** bone + nail composite, flexural rigidity EI_1 = E_b * I_b + E_n * I_n
- **Region 2 (bone only):** distal to the nail tip, flexural rigidity EI_2 = E_b * I_b

At the transition (nail tip), there is a **stiffness discontinuity**: the curvature must jump to maintain moment equilibrium across the interface.

### 5.2 Stiffness Values

| Region | EI (N*mm^2) | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Region 1 (nailed) | 4.181e+08 | E_b * I_b + E_n * I_n |
| Region 2 (bone only) | 3.391e+08 | E_b * I_b |
| **Stiffness ratio EI_1/EI_2** | **1.23** | Nailed region is 1.2x stiffer |

### 5.3 Curvature Jump at Nail Tip

Under a representative bending moment M at the transition:

$$\kappa_1 = \frac{M}{EI_1}, \quad \kappa_2 = \frac{M}{EI_2}$$

$$\Delta\kappa = \kappa_2 - \kappa_1 = M\left(\frac{1}{EI_2} - \frac{1}{EI_1}\right)$$

### 5.4 Short vs. Long Nail Comparison

A **short/mid-length nail** places the transition in a high-moment region (subtrochanteric). A **long, curved nail** pushes the transition distally where the bending moment is lower.

| Scenario | M at tip (N*mm) | kappa_1 (1/mm) | kappa_2 (1/mm) | Delta_kappa (1/mm) | eps_lat,tip (%) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Short nail (tip in high-M zone) | 80640 | 1.93e-04 | 2.38e-04 | 4.50e-05 | 0.3092 |
| Long curved nail (tip in low-M zone) | 48384 | 1.16e-04 | 1.43e-04 | 2.70e-05 | 0.1855 |

**Key finding:** The long curved nail reduces the curvature jump by **40%** and the distal-tip strain by the same proportion, simply by moving the transition to a region of lower bending moment.

### 5.5 Clinical Significance

- The curvature jump Delta_kappa creates a **stress riser** at the nail tip, which is a recognized site of peri-prosthetic fracture [15], [19].
- The stiffness ratio of 1.2x means the bone-only region must bend 1.2x more per unit moment than the nailed region.
- A **longer, bow-matched nail** mitigates this by:
  - Moving the transition distally (lower M).
  - Matching the bone curvature (no anterior contact stress along the nail length).
  - Reducing the effective Delta_kappa at the tip.

## 6. Strain and Fatigue Perspective

### 6.1 Strain Calculation

Lateral cortex strain from the combined (sag + frontal) stress:

$$\varepsilon_{\text{lat}} = \frac{\sigma_{\text{lat,total}}}{E_b}$$

Yield strain (literature): epsilon_y = 0.80%

### 6.2 Fatigue Endurance Limit

For mid-diaphyseal femoral cortical bone, the fatigue endurance limit at ~10^6 cycles is approximately 40-60% of sigma_u,t [6], [7].

Using a conservative 45% of sigma_u,t = 133 MPa:

**sigma_endurance = 45% x 133 = 59.9 MPa**

### 6.3 Results

| Case | sigma_total (MPa) | epsilon_lat (%) | FOS_yield | FOS_fatigue | epsilon_lat / epsilon_y |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bone only | 67.72 | 0.3984 | 1.48 | 0.88 | 0.498 |
| Straight nail | 55.07 | 0.3240 | 1.82 | 1.09 | 0.405 |
| Curved nail | 57.03 | 0.3355 | 1.75 | 1.05 | 0.419 |

### 6.4 Interpretation

- All strains are well below the yield strain (0.80%), with epsilon_lat / epsilon_y ratios of 49.8% – 41.9%.
- **Bone only** has FOS_fatigue = 0.88 (< 1.0), meaning the un-nailed femur is already at risk of fatigue failure under combined loading — consistent with AFF occurring in intact bone. Both nailed cases achieve FOS_fatigue > 1.0.
- However, in AFF patients with **bisphosphonate-suppressed remodeling** and accumulated microdamage, the effective fatigue endurance limit may be substantially lower, potentially reducing these safety margins.
- The key insight is that **even modest stress reductions translate into meaningful fatigue life extensions**, because the S-N curve for cortical bone follows an inverse power law: small decreases in stress amplitude produce disproportionately large increases in cycles to failure [6], [7].

## 7. Synthesis and Design Implications

### 7.1 Multi-Objective Scorecard

| Metric | Straight Nail | Curved Nail | Winner | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| sigma_lat,sag (MPa) | 12.45 | 14.41 | Straight | NA shift reduces c_lat |
| sigma_lat,total (sag+front, MPa) | 55.07 | 57.03 | Straight | Frontal plane equalizes |
| Compatibility index C | 0.146 | 0.000 | Curved | C = 0 is ideal |
| Tip curvature jump | Same (geometry-dependent) | Reduced (longer nail) | Curved | Lower M at distal tip |
| FOS_fatigue | 1.09 | 1.05 | Straight | Both > 1.0 |
| Anterior cortex contact risk | High (C >> 0) | None (C = 0) | Curved | Primary clinical concern |
| Asian female safety | C = 0.282 | C = 0.000 | Curved | Mismatch amplified |

### 7.2 Key Takeaways

1. **Frontal-plane bending dominates.** The coronal moment from hip offset is ~2.6x the sagittal bow moment. Once frontal bending is included, the straight nail's beam-level advantage over the curved nail is marginal (55.1 vs 57.0 MPa total).

2. **The compatibility index reveals the true risk.** The straight nail has C = 0.146 (nominal) to 0.282 (Asian female), meaning the nail centroid sits 1.9–3.7 mm off the canal centerline. This drives anterior cortex contact and localized stress concentrations that beam theory cannot capture.

3. **Stiffness discontinuity at the nail tip** creates a curvature jump of 4.50e-05 1/mm. A longer, curved nail pushes this transition to a lower-moment region, reducing tip strain by ~40%.

4. **Un-nailed bone fails the fatigue criterion** (FOS_fatigue = 0.88 < 1.0) under combined loading, which is consistent with AFF occurring in intact femora. Both nailed cases bring FOS_fatigue above 1.0, but margins are thin — especially relevant for AFF patients with bisphosphonate-suppressed remodeling and reduced effective endurance limits.

5. **Design recommendation:** A **long, patient-specific curved nail** matching the individual femoral ROC offers:
   - C = 0 (no curvature mismatch, no anterior impingement)
   - Comparable beam-level stress performance
   - Reduced stiffness discontinuity at the tip
   - Greatest benefit for high-risk patients (Asian females, excessive bow)
