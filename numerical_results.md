## 6. Key Takeaways

1. **Bone only** has the highest lateral cortex tensile stress — bow-induced bending dominates over axial compression.
2. Both nailed cases reduce the axial compression in the bone (from 4.7 to 1.7 MPa) by sharing load with the stiffer nail.
3. The **straight nail** shifts the composite NA by ȳ = 1.20 mm toward the tension side, which *reduces* c_lat and thus lowers the beam-level bending stress at the lateral cortex. This gives the straight nail the lowest σ_lat in this simplified model.
4. The **curved nail** keeps the NA centered (ȳ = 0) and adds pure bending stiffness (I_comp = 24597 vs I_b = 19947 mm⁴), reducing σ_lat by **5.0%** relative to bone only.
5. All cases have comfortable FOS: bone = 6.59, straight = 8.03, curved = 6.94.
6. Monte Carlo 95th-percentile stresses: bone = 28.8, straight = 18.7, curved = 25.1 MPa — all well below σ_allow = 50 MPa for walking loads.

## 7. Important Model Limitations

This is a **simplified 2D composite-beam model** capturing only bow-induced eccentric compression. It does **not** capture:

1. **Anterior cortex contact/impingement forces**: a straight nail in a curved bone creates point contact at the anterior cortex, which is the primary clinical concern with nail-bone mismatch [12], [15], [19], [20]. These localized stresses can exceed cortical bone strength and cause perforation.
2. **Coronal-plane (medial-lateral) bending**: the hip joint moment arm (~50–60 mm medial offset) produces the dominant bending moment at the subtrochanteric level, causing much higher lateral cortex tensions than the sagittal bow alone.
3. **Muscle forces and 3D loading**: abductor muscles, IT band, and other soft tissues significantly alter the stress state.
4. **Stress concentrations**: at screw holes, nail tip, and nail-bone contact points.
5. **Fatigue and microdamage accumulation**: AFF is a stress fracture that develops over millions of gait cycles, not a single overload event.

**The curved nail's primary clinical advantage** is in eliminating the **anterior cortex perforation risk** and distributing contact forces along the nail length — benefits that this beam-level model does not quantify. A full 3D FEA with contact modeling would be needed to capture these effects.
